RESEARCH

CORE IDEA

My scientific object of interest are interactions; to be specific - interactions in biological systems. What I find most interesting is that the diversity and complexity of life contradict the fact that it is based on a small amount of basic blocks – e.g. the interactions between only 20 amino acids determine the structure of a protein. Same, its function and dynamics. The arising question is why then it’s so difficult to characterize a biological system? Because the interactions between components is a very complex phenomenon.

Interactions allow molecules to change shape and form complexes. They can be divided into two groups – covalent and non-covalent types. Covalent interactions we can call a chemical bond. They hold atoms together within amino acids and amino acids within proteins, being responsible for their so called primary structure. However, the forces that really drive biology are non-covalent interactions; individually they are weak, but collectively they have significant influence on a structure. They are responsible for the secondary and the tertiary structure of proteins. Also they contribute to the specificity of interactions between molecules. To explore their complex aspects I approached them with two strategies: I have worked on different projects and I have combined different methods.

In modern biology, research is an indirect science with reductionism at its foundation. It operates on isolated systems and simple observations, measuring the response of a stimulus to a molecule. As a result we get a landscape representation, a model. To broaden and sharpen this landscape we use – or even better – combine different methods. Biophysical methods help us to understand how a collection of particles interact to maintain life. Alternatively, or as a complement we use computational methods - they look for patterns and algorithms, extrapolating practical results where laboratory techniques are powerless or too expensive. With proper visualization tools and widespread access to technical servers and databases, computational biology has become very potent and interesting field. Nonetheless, the most accurate results are always obtained by combining both, biophysical and computational methods.

Protein-ligand interactions I have studied on the example of heme pathway enzymes and protein-protein interactions on the example of connexin complexes.

PBGD

PROJECT I

In the core of the project is the heme pathway. Heme plays essential roles in almost all living organisms and take part on several metabolic processes, including electron transfer during respiration, photosynthesis, and enzyme catalysis. The heme pathway is accomplished by the sequential action of eight enzymes. An impaired function of any of them, produced by a mutation in a sequence, lies in the basis of the rare disease family called porphyrias, which are largely incurable and whose symptoms (neurological complications and/or skin problems) hamper much of the normal life of patients. Structurally detailed mechanism of action of each enzyme would shed the light on unknown steps of the reaction and facilitate towards e.g. porphyria drug exploration. The project is focused on the structural characterization of porphobilinogen deaminase (PBGD), the third out of eight enzymes involved in the heme pathway.

The most interesting and at the same time most difficult to study characteristic of PBGD is that it catalysis four distinct reactions: it forms a linear tetrapyrrole from four units of porphobilinogen using a single active site, with a distinct and yet chemically equivalent bond being form each time. Ten crystals structures of the protein are available in the Protein Data Bank (PDB ID: 3ECR, above) and the existence of four distinct complexes of the enzyme, each containing a separate reaction intermediate, was proven before, however, the structures of subsequent catalytic stages of PBGD that are essential to understand the mechanism of the reaction remain unknown. Trying to fill this gap, I have established and optimized protocols for expressing and purifying wild type and mutant human PBGDs and separating the complexes with the reaction intermediates. My results prove the dynamic character of the enzyme and agree with the previous predictions, yet, recently obtained crystals for two reaction intermediates and the diffraction data I collected allow hope for breakthrough.

CONNEXINS

PROJECT II

Connexins are generally considered to mediate cell-to-cell communication (critical for the maintenance of tissue/organ homeostasis) by forming gap junction channels. The assembly of connexins into channels occurs in two main stages: connexins first oligomerize into hexameric hemichannels prior to reaching the cell surface, then two hemichannels from neighbouring cells dock to generate a complete intercellular channel. Connexins belong to a multigene family with more than twenty classes identified. Although they share similar topology, different types of connexins form different types of channels, with ultimately different biophysical properties of conductance, gating and permeability. This complexity gives rise to functional diversity and broadens the range intercellular communication offered by connexins but it also makes the study of gap junctions more difficult; only one connexin hemichannel structure has been determined to date (PDB ID: 2ZW3, above).

The in silico experiments I have performed (docking and simulations) are based on this structure and the experimental data provided by collaborators - they assembled different channels in cells, with wild-type connexins and with connexins harbouring mutations that give rise to diseases. The analysis of the models in combination with experimental results enabled us to propose a model of connexin interactions that are vital for gap junctions assembly, bringing us closer to a better understanding of their structure-function relationships (manuscripts in preparation).
